How to Come Up with Good Game Ideas

While we all dream of making the next million-dollar indie game, we often overlook the importance of feasibility. It's crucial to ask ourselves: Can I actually finish this game? Many developers who overlook this question find themselves stuck in an endless cycle of development without a clear path to completion, which we call development hell.

"The best game ideas are the ones you can actually finish. Don't get lost in the dream of a perfect game; focus on what you can realistically achieve."

How It Started

After completing a game development course, I faced my first challenge: creating projects to apply what I'd learned and showcase my skills to potential employers. For that, I was advised to create as many small games as possible, be they hyper-casual or clones. But I didn't agree with them; I didn't want to make something even I wouldn't play.

Now, I also had plenty of ideas for next-gen, groundbreaking game lore and mechanics, but let's be real. It's not something I could do alone in a short period. It would take years and might never even see the light of day.

So, I had to make a choice: scale down my ambitions. The best approach is to start small. I set some constraints: I needed to finish it in a week, and it shouldn't be an art-demanding game, as I'm a programmer before anything else. Now, the easy answer was to ask ChatGPT, right? That's what I tried. It gave me a bunch of game ideas that fit my constraints, but I honestly didn't like any of them. Yet, I kept the very first one just because it was the first one xD. It was a platformer, exactly like NinJump, but without the ninja.

It wasn't what I wanted, but after several hours of getting nowhere, it just clicked: what if instead of the player jumping to another wall, the world itself changed its orientation? And that's how The Gravity Flip was born.

While making The Gravity Flip, a puzzle platformer, I recalled a statement from GMTK: "Making puzzles is hard." You have to make sure the puzzle is challenging enough, yet still solvable. One thing developers often overlook is that a puzzle they think is very easy can turn out to be very difficult for players. Managing all that is quite difficult. And yet, it turned out to be a pretty good game with very positive reviews. You can read the reviews here, and if you want to try the game, you can play it here.

The Real Challenge Begins

Now, after completing The Gravity Flip, the real challenge began. The idea for The Gravity Flip had come to me out of nowhere, and that doesn't happen every time. This time, I spent days and days thinking of a new game idea, but I couldn't come up with anything.

The moment I thought of an idea, I would end up expanding it more and more, to the point where it was no longer achievable within my time constraints. This scope creep would eventually lead to development hell.

One of the ideas was very cool: a robot on a spaceship with no internal battery, needing a constant power supply from a wire. However, designing puzzles around the constraint of a non-extendable wire was hard. I couldn't come up with puzzles that presented both a clear challenge and a solution. I spent several days trying to iterate on that idea and design some puzzles, but I still couldn't make it work. The right call was to scrap that idea or adapt it in another way.

This was my approach for a puzzle game, but what if the next idea wasn't a puzzle? What if it depended heavily on character movement? How do we know if it's worth committing to? That's what we'll see in the next segment.

Prototyping

Prototyping is the process of creating the bare minimum of the final product, excluding all the fancy graphics, music, and art. It's not supposed to be a gorgeous game; it's most likely just some blocks moving around, executing the bare minimum of your core mechanic so you can find out how the game feels.

This is a crucial part, as it tells you how the game feels. Even if you pour your heart and soul into making gorgeous graphics and amazing music, if the core gameplay isn't fun, the game won't succeed. It simply won't provide the Fun. This is where many games fail—they just aren't fun. Developers keep working on them, thinking that better graphics or music will eventually make the game fun, but that rarely happens. The purpose of a prototype is to determine if your core idea is fun and to know when to stop, as the saying goes:

Test Fast, Fail Fast, Adjust Fast

What If We Still Don't Get an Idea?

Play more games, especially in the genre and scope you're aiming for. Try mixing various genres to see if you can create a new gem.

Personal Extras

The Gravity Flip was very positively received, which I feel quite lucky about. I didn't expect people to like it that much. I didn't market it, so there wasn't a big audience, but the people I had try the game all liked it very much.

One more thing to note: the levels I created, thinking they were easy, players found to be quite hard. This reminds me of GMTK's remarks about puzzle design in one of his devlogs.

Conclusion

Game design skill comes with experience. You can't expect to make good games right away. There's a saying that

Your first few games are going to fail,
but the key is to make sure you fail forward. Learn from those failures, improve continuously, keep making games, and eventually, you will be a great game designer. I wish you all the best!
